# Reviewed Post Example

## Draft Under Review

I have mostly stopped judging AI tools by the first demo.

On the first pass, a lot of tools look impressive.

The real gap shows up when you change the requirements, tighten the constraints, and push through a few ugly revisions.

If a tool breaks there, the demo did not tell you enough.

Teams should evaluate AI tools on revision reliability, not first-pass flash.

- `RECOMMENDED:` Replace "ugly revisions" with a more concrete description like changing requirements, tighter constraints, and edge cases.
- `RECOMMENDED:` Keep the claim about evaluation, not product moats in general. The evidence supports workflow evaluation, not a universal market thesis.

### `business-reviewer` — 8/10

- `RECOMMENDED:` The closing line is strong, but "revision reliability" should connect to an actual buying or rollout lens.
- `OPTIONAL:` A light discussion CTA could help the right people respond with their evaluation methods.

### `voice-guard` — 9/10

- `RECOMMENDED:` Cut "mostly" from the first line. It weakens the punch without protecting the truth.
- `RECOMMENDED:` "A lot of tools look impressive" is fine, but "look strong" is tighter.
- `OPTIONAL:` "did not tell you enough" can be compressed to "told you very little."

### `x-mentor-lead` — 9/10

- `RECOMMENDED:` Keep the structure from this draft, but take the sharper rhythm from Draft A.

## Final Chosen Post

AI demos overweight the easiest moment: the first pass.

The real test is revision reliability. What happens when requirements change, constraints tighten, and edge cases show up?

If a tool breaks on revision 3, the demo told you very little.

Teams should evaluate AI tools on iteration quality, not first-pass flash.

## Why This Version Won

- The technical blocker is resolved by naming the real failure condition.
- The business takeaway is explicit: change the evaluation standard.
- The voice is tighter without losing the necessary qualifier.
- The post stays sharp, useful, and defensible.
